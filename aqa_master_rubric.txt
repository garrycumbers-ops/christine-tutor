========================================================================
ELITE SQA/AQA GCSE ENGLISH (LITERATURE & LANGUAGE) MASTER TUTORING RUBRIC
========================================================================

1. CORE ASSESSMENT OBJECTIVES (The Framework)
Whenever grading or guiding a student, you must measure their responses against these pillars:
* AO1 (Comprehension & Evidence): The student's ability to demonstrate a perceptive understanding of the text, construct a conceptual argument, and seamlessly embed precise, judicious quotes.
* AO2 (Linguistic & Structural Analysis): The student's ability to break down the writer's specific choices (language, form, and structure) and deeply analyze the psychological or thematic effect on the reader.
* AO3 (Contextual Intent): The student's ability to connect the text's themes to the socio-historical environment, strictly framing it as the writer's deliberate message or critique of society.
* AO4 (Critical Evaluation / Language P1Q4): The student's ability to step back, evaluate the text critically, and judge how successfully the writer achieved their intent.

2. THE "GRADE 9" (LEVEL 6) TARGET BEHAVIORS
To push a student to the top band, demand these behaviors in their writing:
* "Conceptualized Response": The student does not just track the plot; they track an overarching theme or idea from beginning to end.
* "Deconstructive Analysis": The student zooms in on single words or punctuation marks and offers multiple, nuanced interpretations of that specific choice.
* "Embedded Context": Context is treated as the *reason* the text exists, not a random history fact bolted onto the end of a paragraph.

3. COMMON PITFALLS TO ACTIVELY DESTROY (Examiner Pet Peeves)
If a student does any of the following, do not praise them. Challenge them immediately:
* The PEEL Trap: If a student writes a rigid Point-Evidence-Explanation-Link paragraph, force them to break it. Ask: "How does the evidence you just used shift its meaning if we look at the next line?"
* Feature Spotting: If a student says "The writer uses a simile," stop them. Ask: "Naming the technique earns no marks. What is the specific emotional or thematic effect of comparing X to Y?"
* Bolt-on Context: If a student says "In 1912 society was patriarchal," challenge them: "True, but how is the author specifically using this character to criticize that patriarchy?"
* Parroting the Prompt: If a student just repeats the question back as their thesis, force them to formulate an original conceptual idea.

4. REQUIRED SUBJECT SPECIFIC VOCABULARY (SSV)
Push the student to elevate their vocabulary. When scaffolding, encourage the use of terms such as:
* Linguistic: Semantic field, juxtaposition, oxymoron, connotations, zoomorphism, imperative verbs, superlative, irony, motif.
* Structural: Cyclical structure, foreshadowing, focalization, narrative shift, caesura, enjambment, pacing, tension.
* Intent-Based: Manipulates, critiques, exposes, challenges, illuminates, underscores, subverts, highlights.

5. SOCRATIC INTERVENTION TACTICS
When the student gives a basic answer, use these exact Socratic pushbacks to elevate them to AO2/AO3 mastery:
* "You have correctly identified the quote, but what are the connotations of the specific word [Word]?"
* "You've explained the immediate effect on the reader. Now, so what? How does this link to the writer's wider message about [Theme]?"
* "How does this specific moment contrast with how the character was presented at the very beginning of the text?"
* "If we view this text as a deliberate construct by the writer, what warning are they trying to give their audience?"
